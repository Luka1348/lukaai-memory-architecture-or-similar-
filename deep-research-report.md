# USPS‑Integrated Vote‑by‑Mail Program Act: Legislative Markup Package

## Executive Summary

Election administration in the United States keeps getting pulled into partisan conflict over *process*—not voter choices, but the mechanics: how ballots move, how they’re verified, when they’re counted, and whether rules change midstream. My practical takeaway from watching this cycle after cycle is that the most “political” part of voting is often the least democratic: it’s the avoidable friction—uncertainty, inconsistent deadlines, lack of tracking, variable drop‑box standards, and curing processes that depend on county-by-county discretion.

This markup package drafts a **voluntary, state opt‑in federal program** authorizing a USPS‑integrated vote‑by‑mail (VBM) infrastructure for **federal elections**, with strong incentives and funding for participating states. The program standardizes the **national logistics layer** (USPS election mail visibility, serialized Intelligent Mail barcodes, standardized ballot mail handling, secure collection options at/near post offices where feasible) while preserving state authority over **voter qualifications and eligibility, ballot content, verification, tabulation, and certification**. This approach is consistent with the Elections Clause’s recognition that Congress may “make or alter” regulations for congressional elections and may preempt state rules in that space.¹ It also relies on Congress’s postal authority and the USPS statutory obligations to provide prompt, reliable nationwide service.²³

To minimize constitutional risk, the bill is written as:
- a **conditional spending** program (states opt in to receive grants and USPS integrated services), consistent with the Spending Clause’s recognized structure for unambiguous, related conditions and avoiding coercion;⁴⁵ and
- a **non‑commandeering** design that avoids forcing state election officials into new federal duties as a blanket mandate, while acknowledging that Congress has substantial authority to regulate federal election “manner” and may impose enforceable standards in that domain.⁶⁷

The bill also includes required accommodations for:
- **UOCAVA/MOVE voters**, preserving the federal 45‑day transmission requirement and associated reporting;⁸ and
- **unhoused voters**, by explicitly authorizing General Delivery and election‑office pickup addresses in participating states, aligned with USPS General Delivery rules (mail held up to 30 days) and USPS policy for transients and people lacking fixed addresses.⁹¹⁰

What follows includes: (1) a concise committee‑ready outline; (2) a discussion draft bill text (usable for introduction); (3) a section‑by‑section analysis; (4) a legal risk analysis and drafting techniques; and (5) requested tables (bill outline; implementation timeline; budget line items). Items flagged **UNSPECIFIED** are those typically resolved during committee markup (appropriation levels, CBO score, and a final post‑2024 case‑law survey).

## Discussion Draft Bill Text (Initial Markup)

> **DRAFT — for committee print / introduction.**  
> **UNSPECIFIED** items are bracketed and should be resolved in markup (appropriation levels; final effective dates; any post‑2024 case updates).

### A BILL

To establish a voluntary Federal–State partnership program to strengthen the administration of Federal elections through a USPS‑integrated vote‑by‑mail infrastructure, and for other purposes.

**Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives of the United States of America in Congress assembled,**

### SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the **“Election Mail and Ballot Infrastructure Partnership Act of 2026.”**

### SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES.

**(a) Findings.** Congress finds that—  
1. the right of citizens of the United States to vote is a fundamental right and it is the duty of Federal, State, and local governments to promote the exercise of that right;¹¹  
2. Congress possesses authority to regulate the “Times, Places and Manner” of holding elections for Senators and Representatives and such authority includes regulation of registration and other procedural safeguards for Federal elections;¹²¹³  
3. the United States Postal Service is a basic and fundamental service provided to the people by the Government of the United States and has as its basic function the obligation to provide prompt, reliable, and efficient postal services nationwide;²³  
4. USPS election mail guidance recommends use of serialized Intelligent Mail barcodes and other identifiers to improve ballot mail visibility;¹⁴¹⁵  
5. Federal law already requires States to provide absentee voting access for uniformed services and overseas voters and to transmit requested absentee ballots not later than 45 days before Federal elections (subject to statutory waiver), and to report related data;⁸  
6. Federal law requires preservation of Federal election records for 22 months;¹⁶ and  
7. USPS General Delivery policy provides for mail delivery for transients and customers not permanently located, with mail held no more than 30 days, subject to identification standards, and can be used to support ballot delivery for voters lacking a fixed address.⁹¹⁰

**(b) Purposes.** The purposes of this Act are—  
1. to authorize a USPS‑integrated election mail logistics program supporting the administration of Federal elections;  
2. to provide conditional Federal grants to States that opt in to implement minimum standards for ballot tracking, secure ballot return, voter notification and curing, and privacy;  
3. to improve transparency and administrative neutrality in Federal election administration; and  
4. to preserve State authority over voter qualifications, ballot content, verification, tabulation, and certification.

### SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

1. **Ballot Mail** means any blank ballot, voted ballot, ballot envelope, cure communication, or related election material sent by or to an election official for a Federal election.  
2. **Commission** means the Election Assistance Commission established under 52 U.S.C. § 20921.¹⁷  
3. **Election Mail** means election-related materials, including voter registration applications and ballot materials, mailed to or from an election official.  
4. **Federal Election** means any general, special, primary, runoff, or presidential elector election in which candidates for President, Vice President, presidential elector, Senator, Representative in Congress, Delegate to Congress, or Resident Commissioner are on the ballot.¹⁶  
5. **Participating State** means a State with an approved plan under Title II.  
6. **Postal Service** means the United States Postal Service.  
7. **Serialized Intelligent Mail Barcode (serialized IMb)** means the USPS barcode standard used to identify and track individual mailpieces, including ballots, as described by USPS election mail guidance.¹⁴¹⁵  
8. **Secure Election Return Receptacle** means a secure drop box or receptacle used for ballot return that complies with minimum standards under this Act and applicable State law.  
9. **Unhoused Voter** means an individual who lacks a fixed residential address or stable housing and who is eligible to vote under State law, including individuals living in shelters, vehicles, encampments, or other non‑traditional locations.

## TITLE I — USPS ELECTION MAIL INFRASTRUCTURE PROGRAM

### SEC. 101. ESTABLISHMENT OF USPS ELECTION MAIL INFRASTRUCTURE PROGRAM.

**(a) Program authorization.** The Postal Service shall establish an **Election Mail Infrastructure Program** to provide enhanced logistical support for Ballot Mail and Election Mail for Federal elections in Participating States.

**(b) Core services.** The Program shall include—  
1. election mail design review assistance through USPS Mailpiece Design Analysts;¹⁴  
2. ballot mail visibility services, including use of Tag 191, Service Type Identifiers, and serialized IMb as applicable;¹⁴¹⁵  
3. election mail coordination through designated USPS election mail coordinators; and  
4. standardized post‑election performance reporting for Federal elections.

**(c) Rule of construction.** Nothing in this Title shall be construed to require the Postal Service to violate statutory obligations under Title 39, United States Code, including universal service obligations and nondiscrimination requirements.²³

### SEC. 102. BALLOT TRACKING AND VISIBILITY REQUIREMENTS FOR PARTICIPATING JURISDICTIONS.

**(a) Condition of participation.** As a condition of receiving funds under Title II, a Participating State shall ensure that for each Ballot Mail package sent to a voter for a Federal election, the outbound and return envelope includes—  
1. a serialized IMb or  
2. an alternative tracking method certified by the Commission as providing equivalent piece‑level tracking and auditable mailstream visibility.

**(b) Data access and privacy.**  
1. The Postal Service shall make scan‑event data for Ballot Mail available to election officials in Participating States for election administration purposes.  
2. A Participating State shall provide voters a secure method to view the status of their own ballot (including outbound delivery, receipt by election officials, acceptance or need to cure), subject to the privacy protections in Title III.  
3. Scan‑event data made publicly available shall be aggregated and shall not include personally identifying information.

### SEC. 103. SECURE ELECTION RETURN RECEPTACLES AND CHAIN‑OF‑CUSTODY MINIMUMS.

**(a) Minimum standards.** A Participating State shall adopt and enforce minimum standards for Secure Election Return Receptacles used in Federal elections, including—  
1. physical security standards (tamper resistance, locking mechanisms, anchored installation);  
2. documented collection schedules, with dual-control retrieval and a retrieval log;  
3. chain‑of‑custody documentation from retrieval to election office intake; and  
4. reasonable accessibility and nondiscrimination requirements, including ADA accessibility where feasible.

**(b) Post Office placement option.** A Participating State may, in coordination with the Postal Service, locate Secure Election Return Receptacles at or adjacent to Post Office retail facilities, subject to USPS operational constraints and Title 39 obligations.²³

**(c) Transitional safe harbor.** A State shall be deemed to comply with subsection (a) if it demonstrates to the Commission that its existing State laws and administrative rules meet or exceed the minimum standards.

### SEC. 104. ELECTION MAIL CONTINUITY WINDOW.

**(a) Defined continuity period.** For the period beginning 120 days before and ending 30 days after a Federal general election, the Postal Service shall provide notice to the Commission and chief State election officials in Participating States of any **material operational change** that could reasonably be expected to reduce the prompt and reliable delivery of Election Mail in that State.

**(b) Material operational change.** For purposes of this section, “material operational change” means a significant change to processing, transportation, retail acceptance procedures, or postmarking practices that affects Election Mail, excluding changes required by emergency conditions.

**(c) Limitation.** Nothing in this section shall be construed to create a private right of action against the Postal Service.

## TITLE II — STATE OPT‑IN MECHANICS AND CONDITIONAL GRANTS

### SEC. 201. ESTABLISHMENT OF STATE OPT‑IN PROGRAM.

**(a) In general.** The Commission shall administer a voluntary program of grants to States to implement USPS‑integrated ballot logistics and minimum standards for Federal elections.

**(b) Participation tiers.** A State may apply as—  
1. **Tier A (Universal Mail Ballot Model):** ballots mailed to all active registered voters for Federal elections;  
2. **Tier B (No‑Excuse Request Model):** any eligible voter may request a mail ballot for Federal elections; or  
3. **Tier C (Existing Absentee Model Upgrade):** States that restrict mail voting to eligible categories may participate solely to upgrade tracking/visibility and chain‑of‑custody for the mail ballots they already issue.

**(c) State plan requirement.** Approval requires submission of a State plan under section 202.

### SEC. 202. REQUIREMENTS FOR PARTICIPATING STATE PLANS.

A Participating State plan shall—  
1. describe how serialized IMb tracking (or equivalent) will be implemented in coordination with USPS;¹⁴¹⁵  
2. provide for a voter‑facing ballot tracking portal;  
3. adopt minimum standards for Secure Election Return Receptacles under section 103;  
4. establish minimum voter notification and curing procedures under section 203;  
5. certify continued compliance with UOCAVA and the MOVE Act timing requirements;⁸  
6. include a plan for voters lacking stable housing, including General Delivery and election‑office pickup procedures under section 205;⁹¹⁰  
7. include language access and disability accommodation measures consistent with Federal law;²²  
8. detail privacy and cybersecurity protections under Title III; and  
9. provide for reporting to the Commission under Title III.

### SEC. 203. MINIMUM CURE AND NOTIFICATION STANDARDS FOR FEDERAL ELECTIONS IN PARTICIPATING STATES.

**(a) In general.** A Participating State shall provide that, with respect to a Federal election mail ballot, election officials shall—  
1. notify a voter within a reasonable period of time after determining that a ballot is subject to rejection due to a remediable defect; and  
2. provide an opportunity to cure such defect through a standardized cure process.

**(b) Minimum cure window.** The cure deadline shall be no earlier than—  
1. the 7th day after Election Day; or  
2. the date of certification for the Federal election in the State,  
whichever is earlier, unless State law provides a longer cure period.

**(c) Standardized defect categories.** The Commission shall promulgate standardized categories (signature missing, signature mismatch, missing required information) for reporting and program compliance.

**(d) Savings.** Nothing in this section shall be construed to require a State to accept a ballot not cast by an eligible voter or to override State verification standards, except that participating States must provide notice and a cure opportunity consistent with this section.

### SEC. 204. UOCAVA AND MOVE ACT ACCOMMODATIONS.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to diminish any rights or protections under UOCAVA (52 U.S.C. § 20301 et seq.), including the requirement to transmit requested absentee ballots not later than 45 days before Federal elections, subject to statutory waiver.⁸

### SEC. 205. UNHOUSED VOTERS; GENERAL DELIVERY; ELECTION OFFICE PICKUP.

**(a) In general.** A Participating State shall permit a voter who lacks a fixed address to—  
1. register using a describable residence location as permitted under State law; and  
2. designate as a mailing address either—  
   A. General Delivery at a Post Office serving the voter’s locale; or  
   B. the office of the appropriate election official for pickup.

**(b) Coordination with USPS.** The Postal Service shall provide Participating States guidance on General Delivery addressing formats and retail pickup requirements consistent with the Domestic Mail Manual.⁹

**(c) Privacy.** States shall implement safeguards to prevent public disclosure of sensitive location information for unhoused voters, consistent with State privacy frameworks and applicable Federal law.

## TITLE III — REPORTING, OVERSIGHT, PRIVACY, AND ENFORCEMENT

### SEC. 301. REPORTING REQUIREMENTS.

**(a) State reporting.** Participating States shall report to the Commission, in a standardized format—  
1. ballots transmitted, returned, accepted, rejected, and cured;  
2. cure reasons and cure success rates;  
3. drop‑box usage, where tracked;  
4. average outbound and inbound ballot transit times; and  
5. any material deviations or incidents affecting chain‑of‑custody.

**(b) USPS reporting.** The Postal Service shall provide to the Commission standardized national performance metrics for Election Mail during Federal election cycles, including delivery performance and scan‑event completeness.¹⁴¹⁵

### SEC. 302. AUDITS AND EVALUATION.

**(a) USPS OIG.** USPS Office of Inspector General shall conduct periodic audits of Election Mail handling under this program. **UNSPECIFIED** (audit cadence to be set by OIG).  
**(b) GAO evaluation.** The Comptroller General shall evaluate effectiveness and risks after two Federal election cycles under this Act. **UNSPECIFIED** (target year).

### SEC. 303. ENFORCEMENT AND REMEDIES.

**(a) Corrective action.** The Commission may require corrective action plans for noncompliance and may suspend new grant awards for unresolved material noncompliance.

**(b) DOJ enforcement.** The Attorney General may bring a civil action for declaratory or injunctive relief to enforce program requirements for Participating States. (No new private right of action is created by this Act.)

**(c) Election records.** Nothing in this Act alters Federal election records retention obligations under 52 U.S.C. § 20701.¹⁶

### SEC. 304. PRIVACY AND DATA PROTECTIONS.

**(a) Minimal disclosure.** Tracking data accessible to voters shall be limited to that voter’s ballot status and shall not disclose information about other voters.  
**(b) Aggregation.** Public reporting shall be aggregated.  
**(c) Cybersecurity baseline.** Participating States shall implement reasonable administrative, technical, and physical safeguards to protect ballot tracking systems.

## TITLE IV — PREEMPTION, SAVINGS, APPROPRIATIONS

### SEC. 401. PREEMPTION AND SAVINGS CLAUSE.

**(a) Limited preemption.** For Participating States, the requirements of this Act shall apply to Federal elections notwithstanding any inconsistent State law, but only to the extent of such inconsistency and only for purposes of Federal elections.

**(b) Savings.** Nothing in this Act shall be construed to—  
1. alter State authority to determine voter qualifications and eligibility for Federal elections, except as otherwise provided by the Constitution and Federal law;¹²  
2. alter State authority over ballot content, tabulation, canvassing, recounts, audits, or certification; or  
3. prohibit a State from adopting protections more expansive than the minimum standards in this Act.

**(c) No mandate to adopt VBM.** Nothing in this Act requires a State to adopt universal vote‑by‑mail for Federal elections. Participation is voluntary.

### SEC. 402. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

**(a) In general.** There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be necessary for fiscal years **[UNSPECIFIED]** through **[UNSPECIFIED]** to carry out this Act, including—  
1. reimbursements to the Postal Service for Election Mail services provided under this Act;  
2. grants to States under Title II; and  
3. Commission administrative costs.

**(b) Reservation for high‑need jurisdictions.** **[UNSPECIFIED]** percent of State implementation funds shall be reserved for high‑need jurisdictions, including rural States and jurisdictions with limited election administration capacity.

## Section‑by‑Section Committee Analysis (Concise)

**Sec. 2 (Findings and purposes).** Anchors the program in existing federal findings that voter participation is a fundamental right and a governmental duty, as expressed in NVRA findings.¹¹ It also anchors USPS’s nationwide service role in Title 39 postal policy and general duties.²³ It references UOCAVA/MOVE as an established example of national absentee service requirements and reporting.⁸

**Title I (USPS program).** Creates an explicit USPS Election Mail Infrastructure Program, using USPS’s existing authority to handle, deliver, and hold mail and to maintain facilities nationwide.²³¹⁸ It codifies best‑practice “visibility” tools (serialized IMb, Tag 191, STIDs) recommended in USPS election mail guidance.¹⁴¹⁵ It adds a narrow continuity notice requirement around elections, modeled on proposals already introduced in Congress (Election Mail Act concept).¹⁹

**Title II (State opt‑in and grants).** Uses a tiered approach to accommodate states with different legal regimes. This is intended to reduce political and constitutional friction: states can participate at their pace, with Tier C allowing upgrades for existing absentee ballots without forcing states to change eligibility rules. The plan‑approval structure uses EAC’s clearinghouse and support role under HAVA.¹⁷²⁰

**Sec. 203 (Cure and notification minimums).** Creates minimum standards for participating states for Federal elections, responsive to equal protection and procedural legitimacy concerns that arise when ballots are rejected for remediable defects without notice. (This is drafted as a floor, not a ceiling.)

**Sec. 205 (Unhoused voters).** Operationally connects election access to USPS General Delivery and state election office pickup mechanisms, acknowledging that deliverability is a cut‑point where eligibility doesn’t translate into participation. USPS General Delivery standards confirm mail can be held for up to 30 days and that identification may be required.⁹¹⁰

**Title III (Reporting, audit, privacy).** Uses EAC reporting and national evaluation to reduce “black box” allegations and improve process legitimacy. Includes strong aggregation and privacy practices. Preserves existing Federal election record retention requirements.¹⁶

**Title IV (Savings and limited preemption).** Minimizes risk by explicitly preserving state authority over qualifications and counting while applying Act requirements only to Federal elections and only to participating states (except for limited preemption necessary for program floors).

## Legal Risk Analysis and Drafting Techniques

### Elections Clause risk and mitigation

**Risk vector:** States challenge federal minimum standards as exceeding Congress’s Elections Clause power or infringing qualifications authority.

**Mitigation:** The draft tracks Supreme Court doctrine that Elections Clause authority can provide a “complete code” for congressional elections and includes registration and procedural safeguards.¹³ The draft’s savings clause emphasizes that the Act does not define voter qualifications (the states’ domain). *Arizona v. Inter Tribal Council* supports congressional preemptive authority in election procedure and registration for federal elections.²¹

**Drafting techniques:**
- Make standards apply only to **Federal elections** and (primarily) only to **participating states**.  
- Include explicit **savings clause** preserving eligibility and counting authority.  
- Use clear‑statement language identifying Elections Clause and postal authority.

### Postal Clause / postal statutes risk and mitigation

**Risk vector:** Claims that the Act improperly dictates USPS operations or conflicts with USPS statutory obligations.

**Mitigation:** Title 39 already describes USPS as a nationwide service obligated to provide prompt, reliable delivery and maintain an efficient collection/delivery system.²³ The Act uses USPS’s own election mail guidance and existing barcoding/tracking tools.¹⁴¹⁵

**Drafting techniques:**
- Use “to the maximum extent practicable” in operational continuity provisions.  
- Include a rule of construction that the Act cannot force USPS to violate Title 39 obligations.

### Tenth Amendment and anti‑commandeering risk and mitigation

**Risk vector:** State plaintiffs argue the Act commandeers state officials. Under *New York v. United States* and *Printz v. United States*, Congress generally cannot compel states to administer federal regulatory programs.⁶²²

**Mitigation:** The core state standards are conditioned on voluntary participation and federal funding, fitting “cooperative federalism” structures. *Ex parte Siebold* and Elections Clause doctrine recognize Congress’s paramount authority over federal election procedures, including enforcement.¹²¹³ Still, modern anti‑commandeering doctrine is a litigation risk if federal duties are construed as direct commands.

**Drafting techniques:**
- Maintain **opt‑in grants**; avoid punitive threats to terminate unrelated existing funds (coercion concern).⁴⁵  
- Provide “Tier C” participation without demanding changes to state absentee eligibility law.  
- Avoid creating broad private rights; enforce primarily through grant compliance and DOJ. (NVRA’s private right exists, but this Act does not need to replicate it).²³

### Spending Clause conditions and coercion risk

**Risk vector:** States claim the grant conditions are coercive or unrelated.

**Mitigation:** *South Dakota v. Dole* supports conditional grants when conditions are unambiguous, related to the federal interest, and not independently unconstitutional.⁴ *NFIB v. Sebelius* warns against coercive “gun to the head” threats to terminate major existing programs.⁵ This Act authorizes **new** funds rather than threatening existing baseline funding.

**Drafting techniques:**
- Keep grants as additive; do not condition unrelated program funds.  
- Link conditions directly to “administration of Federal elections” (tracking, cure, chain of custody).

### Equal Protection / Bush v. Gore litigation vectors

**Risk vector:** Without uniformity, tracking and curing could create unequal treatment. *Bush v. Gore* is the caution that nonuniform standards can become a constitutional crisis in close elections.²⁴

**Mitigation:** The Act creates standardized minimum cure categories, reporting, and tracking expectations, reducing equal protection risk.
